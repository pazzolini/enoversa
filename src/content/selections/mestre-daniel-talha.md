### The Memory of Clay

In Vila Alva, the *Talha* (amphora) is not a trend; it is a religion. The **XXVI Talhas** project is a beautiful reclamation of this ancient methodology, and the 'Mestre Daniel' cuvée is its purest expression.

This is wine made as it was 2,000 years ago: grapes enter the clay pot, fermentation happens spontaneously, and the wine rests on the skins and lees until St. Martin’s Day. No pumps, no electricity, no filtration, and no makeup. It is a direct link to the Roman history of the Alentejo landscape.

#### The Profile
This is a white wine that behaves like a red. The extended skin contact gives it structure, grip, and a profound amber hue.

* **Texture:** This is the highlight. It coats the mouth with a chalky, granular sensation derived from the clay contact. It feels "grounded" and substantial.
* **Notes:** Dried apricot, orange peel, fennel, and a savory, almost dusty earthiness. It is oxidative but fresh—a paradox that only true Talha wines seem to master.

#### The Narrative
Drinking Mestre Daniel is an exercise in archaeology. You are tasting the vessel as much as the grape (Antão Vaz, Perrum, Roupeiro). It is a reminder that wine was originally a food source, a staple of sustenance, rather than a luxury good.

It requires patience. Open it, let it breathe, and watch how the oxidation evolves from "bruised apple" to "complex honeycomb" over the course of an hour.

**Pairing:** Partridge stew or strong sheep cheese (Queijo de Serpa).  
**Mood:** Contemplative and grounded.
